If your distinction between the 'how' and the 'what' of the view of giving
is designed to highlight the issue of meaning, then I agree. If I see two
people grasping an object and releasing it from one to the other, that, I
take it, is the 'what' you are referring to. Viewing this 'what' as an
act of giving is the 'how' I might view it. Similarly, two people
emitting sounds at each other and responding mutually in body language in
a kind of dance of the eyes, gestures and facial expressions, is a 'what'.
Viewing this as a "conversation" is a 'how'. If this captures what you
have in mind, then my argument is that a 'how' that captures the
meaningfulness of the act to the actors requires some tertium quid beyond
the physical dynamic.

There are several issues getting confused here. One, there might be some things 'beyond the physical dynamic' which need to be taken into consideration in describing the world. Two, there is an inherent need for ternary relations. These two issues have nothing to do with one another. The first is deserving of long discussion, but the second, taken at face value, is just plain wrong. Anything that can be said using n-ary relations can be said using only binary relations, if the 'saying' is allowed to use the normal apparatus of modern logic, ie in particular if it can use existential quantifiers and conjunction. This in fact follows from Peirce's own argument for the irreducibility of triadicity, ironically enough, since Peirce showed that anything could be said using binary relations plus ternary identity; but in modern logic, ternary identity can be defined as follows:

TI(x,y,z) <=> (x=y & y=z)

Now, one might have objections to this translation on various grounds (it fails to provide an 'adequate analysis' (Awbrey), or it misses the essential triadicity of the use of a name three times (Sowa), or it fails to 'cover' identity properly, or whatever) but the fact remains, that it exists, and it preserves truth. And by so existing, it establishes the elementary fact that, to repeat, anything that can be said using n-ary relations, can be said using binary relations. So it is pointless to simply *deny* this fact. What might be worth doing is stating the thesis that you believe in more carefully, so that it has a snowball's chance in hell of actually being true, rather than simply reiterating a logical absurdity as though it were Holy writ.

Though this is not really an email topic, let me try a quick, somewhat
fanciful example.

Suppose we drew a line and cut it at an arbitrary point, and at the cut
there exploded an infinity of possibilities not only for resolving the
point into a continuum of infinitesimals, but for the 'how' (in your
sense) of viewing each infinitesimal. Is it useful to construe one as
opposed to another infinitesimal variation as defining a distinct
'possible world'? Or would we want a less inflated vocabulary for
handling these variations?

There is a subtle point here about logical interpretations. A theory might have models in which there are large infinities, without the theory having a large vocabulary. Indeed in many respects the inverse is true: it takes a large vocabulary to *rule out* the models with large cardinalities. For some lovely examples of this see Van Benthem, J. F. A. K. (1983) The Logic Of Time, where quite simple temporal theories are shown to have all kinds of outrageously infinite 'nonstandard' models. For example, suppose the theory simply refers to a partial ordering of timepoints, with every time having a unique past but possibly more than one future. This tiny axiomatic theory has models in which every timepoint has an uncountable infinity of immediate temporal successors. Now suppose that the theory insists that time is totally ordered, so the future is unique; this can be done by adding one simple axiom. This theory has models in which infinitely long series of times can be interpolated between any two times. And so on.

So questions about what possibilities are contemplated by a theory as what might be called ontological alternatives, and questions about the vocabulary of an ontology, need to be sharply distinguished from each other.
