Ciao (again) Aldo,

I like (and agree with) your comments on language/culture etc (copied
below). But there is one point that seems to be missed. This is the
difference in context between spoken and written language.

You mention law and commerce in relation to ordinary language. As I
understand it law and commerce are related to written language - indeed, one
thesis is that the needs of commerce led to the creation of written
language - the first writing was accounting. Modern law (see for example
Clancy - From Memory to Written Record) and commerce are closely tied into
written text. And written text has a 4-d rather then 3-D context. I speak
and you hear here and now (ignoring modern technology for the moment) but I
can write something over a period, revising it many times - and it can be
read is several different times and places.

Of course, language evolved to be spoken here and now - and for most of its
history it was used in that way. So there is a 3-D bias built into it.

However, I think this explains the early 17th century scientists (at least
in England) distrust of ordinary language as a useful tool for science. It
also explains why various communities developed other forms of storing
knowledge. Scientists and mathematics, engineers and their drawings,
accountants and their balance sheets and maybe knowledge engineers and their
logic (?).

Regards,
Chris

Hi all,
just to complicate things out there:

1) there aren't only bodies and events: think about places, time
spans, abstract entities, qualities, ... continuant/occurrent gives a
chance to classify all this stuff with one criterion (not the only
one available of course);

2) body/event (better object/event?) is a VERY intuitive distinction,
but intuition is not magic and it is not simply a linguistic
convention: it is a  naturalistic and cultural product, derived from
perception and interaction habits that our species developed (none
familiar with Gibson's 'affordances'?). No walker will pay attention
to stumbling over an event like 'running', but he probably would to
stumbling over a 'running rabbit' as well as an astonished 'static
rabbit';

3) language structure depends on such intuitions. Nominalisation does
not destroy the underlying conceptualization. It is only a way of
making language more flexible in order to meet the needs of complex
abstract thinking. Indeed, more 'practice-oriented' languages like
early Latin do not show frequent nominalizations. Most 3-d
ontologists keep naming events with nominalizations ..

4) maybe in areas of human culture that heavily commit on everyday
language assumptions (including (e-)commerce, law, and even
biomedicine), would a strong 4-d perdurantist ontology have hard
times?


>(snip) Simpler in spades. It is of course *possible* to choose one
>way of looking at a thing like a flame or a wave, and simply declare
>that this is the 'proper' view, and thereby place it firmly into one
>of the rigid classifications of an endurantist philosophical
>classification. Ive had this debate with Nicola, and he has a sure
>hand at doing this. However, this gives rise to ghastly problems
>when one actually gets down to the nitty-gritty axiomatic details.
>In order to reason about something like a flame, one needs to
>sometimes think of it as enduring like an object, keeping its
>identity through time, and sometimes to think of it as a dynamic
>process. Often, as for example expressing something like 'a gas flow
>rate which produces a manifold pressure below the critical value
>will cause flame loss and require re-ignition within five seconds to
>avoid an automatic emergency call', quite simple pieces of physical
>knowledge require that these different 'views' are interleaved very
>rapidly, sometimes in the same axiom. (For another example, try
>formalizing the reasoning behind the conclusion that one should not
>drink from a cup which has been dipped into a sewer and then filled
>with clean water.) For an endurantist like Nicola, the only option
>is to introduce *two* things here: the flame-as-process and the
>flame-as-entity (like distinguishing a person from their life), and
>to say one type of things about one of them and the other about the
>other of them, and have elaborate axioms connecting them together in
>appropriate ways. For example, we might have to say explicitly that
>for any time during the lifetime, it is true at that time that the
>life is being (notice the use of the continuous tense, suitable only
>for processes) the life of the thing. And this is possible, but it
>is all UNNECESSARY. Why should we be forced into such torture, to
>satisfy Nicola's (or Peter's, or John Sowa's, or Niles's, or mine
>for that matter) philosophical/linguistic feelings about what needs
>to be distinguished from what?

Why unnecessary? after all, you should not have all concepts in all
contexts and/or from all viewpoints. Anyway, in a not-so-naïve
ontology of chemical processes from an everyday life viewpoint, a
flame is an amount of glowing gas that is one of the products of a
combustion process.
In your example, you might not interleave views within the same
axiom, firstly because you can hardly describe that concept with one
axiom only, secondly because re-ignition, flow-rate, and loss are all
processes that concern amounts of gas. In a 4-d conceptualization of
your example one could end up creating many parts of a 4-d flame,
each accounting for the process at hand ..


>BTW, to elaborate on another message I sent in reply to Niles, many
>of these categorizations derive from natural language usage. NL
>structures (case distinctions, the verb/noun distinction, and such
>things as temporal aspect) often do suggest exactly this kind of
>distinction, and have motivated much of the relevant philsophical
>work that underpins it (such as the absurd doctrine that all
>occurrents can only be individuated by a connecting continuant.) But
>it is vitally important to bear in mind that NL is NOT an ontology
>language. It evolved for human communication in real time, between
>creatures living in a 'present'. It is a fundamentally tensed,
>indexical language used for highly context-dependent  communication
>betwseen intelligent agents about an essentially 3-d world. Our
>ontologies, to be any use at all, need to stay true throughout time;
>they are not in a tensed or indexical langauge; they cannot rely on
>the intelligence or quick-wittedness of the reader,  and they are
>being used largely to support reasoning, not to communicate
>immediate facts about the local circumstances of a speech act. This
>is basically why linguistic intutions do more harm than good in
>practical ontology design.
>In any case, I would add, more up-to-date empirical work on
>languages has often shown that the categorisations of earlier
>analyses are simplistic. English nominalisation, in particular,
>almost destroys the noun/verb distinction in practice.
>

I agree that natural language is biased towards "context-dependent
communication between intelligent agents about an essentially 3-d
world" (nice headline!), and it is just for this reason that I
believe that an ontology that cannot easily map basic linguistic
distinctions can be at odds in some domains.
But I am not saying that we should directly buy temporal aspects and
noun/verb usage of a language: what counts is the conceptualization
underlying linguistic structures. Actually, comparative linguists
know that linguistic structures could be very different between
different languages, even affecting the 'style of thinking' of native
speakers. Nonetheless, such variety is neither unlimited nor
accidental.
Still more beef: one of the applications where a top-level ontology
seems really useful is the construction of cross-wordnets (e.g.
Eurowordnet evolution).
