Adam,

>Josiane,
>   I think we may not be communicating completely.  I agree that the 
>context of each sentence is different, but the semantics of the relation 
>"on" seems the same in each.
It depends on what does semantic mean.  There is a level of semantics where
you are. I know this approach. But it does not give anything more than what
has already been done in analysis of verbal reports, and especially it leads
to contradictions between the different verbal reports of one participant as
the criticism of introspection has already shown.
If you agree that the context of each sentence is different, where is the
difference ? Is there an infinitely high number of contexts ? so that
nothing has a stable meaning ?
My position is that when stable meanings i.e. concepts are recalled from
memory in a specific task, these meanings are distorted. The study of these
distorsions can give hints upon the construction of new concepts.
My assumption is that one can formalized the ways in which concepts are
distorted. I assume that it corresponds with the characterization of classes
of contexts that it would be very interesting for you to integrate
to your ontology.

>Now, it occurs to me that you may have had a different point, which is that 
>"on" refers to a slightly different spatial relation in some of the 
>utterances below.  A box may be "on" another box.  A box may also be "on" a 
>boat but that use of "on" actually means "contained in".  We might also 
>envision a carnival "ring toss" game in which a ring may be "on" a peg 
>although it really is surrounding the peg.  Similarly, I may put "on" a 
>shirt which really means that it completely surrounds my torso.
>
>As a diversion, I'm trying to learn a bit of the Philippino language 
>Tagalog in preparation for a trip there.  Tagalog has a very general 
>preposition "sa" which can variously mean "on", "to", "towards", and "on 
>top of".  This relates to an earlier discussion about how natural languages 
>can lead us astray when it comes to creating good ontologies.  We must be 
>careful to separate ontology and language as there are many concepts which 
>are needed in any rich ontology which don't correspond directly to any one 
>word in a human language.
>
But it would be better if you have had formalized contexts. Such a situation
as learning Tagalog would surely strike you upon contextual composants which
could be considered and formalized.
